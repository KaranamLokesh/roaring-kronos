\begin{abstract}
Financial foundation models such as Kronos~\cite{kronos2026} use Binary
Spherical Quantization~(BSQ)~\cite{bsq2024} to convert continuous
candlestick~(OHLCVA) bars into discrete tokens drawn from a vocabulary
of size~$2^{K}$. On real market data, this vocabulary is staggeringly
sparse and regime-clustered: in a~17{,}352-bar BTC-USD hourly corpus
spanning two years, only~417 of the~1{,}048{,}576 possible tokens
(0.04\%) ever appear, and the top-49 tokens cover~80\% of all bars.
Existing pretraining pipelines treat every position uniformly, wasting
capacity on dominant ``chop'' tokens while undersampling the rare
shock tokens that drive tail risk. We apply Roaring
Bitmaps~\cite{roaring2016} --- the standard data structure for
clustered integer sets --- to BSQ token posting lists, yielding three
contributions: \emph{(i)}~a stratified shock-bar dataloader that
increases per-batch rare-token coverage by~27\% with zero measurable
training-time overhead on an NVIDIA~A10; \emph{(ii)}~corpus-scale
rare-event lookup that is~11.4$\times$ faster than linear scan and
scales sub-linearly to the full~12-billion-record Kronos corpus; and
\emph{(iii)}~a hierarchical co-occurrence diagnostic revealing that
52~of~163 active coarse~(s1) tokens have a fully deterministic fine
(s2) token --- a structural property of the trained BSQ tokenizer that
the original paper never measured. In a fine-tune backtest on
BTC~hourly data, our stratified sampler achieves the best
single-month~RankIC of any variant (April~2026, +0.195) and reduces
maximum drawdown by~2.7~percentage points versus uniform sampling,
with~no aggregate signal-quality penalty.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%==============================================================================

The recent~Kronos foundation model~\cite{kronos2026} adapts the
decoder-only Transformer architecture~\cite{vaswani2017} to financial
candlestick forecasting by tokenizing each OHLCVA bar into a discrete
symbol drawn from a large vocabulary. Pretraining is performed on a
corpus of~12 billion bars sourced from over~45 global
exchanges. Tokenization uses Binary Spherical
Quantization~(BSQ)~\cite{bsq2024}, which projects an encoded bar
representation onto the unit hypersphere and applies element-wise
sign($\cdot$) to produce a~$K$-bit binary code. Kronos uses~$K=20$
total bits, split hierarchically into~$s_1=10$ coarse and~$s_2=10$
fine bits, giving an effective vocabulary of size~$2^{20}=1{,}048{,}576$.

In this paper we report two observations about the empirical structure
of BSQ-tokenized financial corpora that, to our knowledge, have not
previously been quantified, and use them to motivate a Roaring
Bitmap-based augmentation of the Kronos pretraining and fine-tuning
pipeline.

\textbf{Observation~1 (extreme vocabulary sparsity).} On
17{,}352~hourly~BTC-USD bars spanning May~2024--May~2026, only
417~of~$2^{20}$ possible tokens (0.04\%) are observed. The top~49
tokens cover~80\% of all bars; the top~10 cover~44.5\%.

\textbf{Observation~2 (regime clustering).} Token occurrences are
strongly autocorrelated: the mean run-length of the coarse~$s_1$ token
is~2.4 bars, with a maximum of~80 consecutive bars (a multi-day
regime). Common tokens form long runs; rare tokens cluster around
shock events.

These two observations together describe exactly the integer-set
structure for which Roaring Bitmaps~\cite{roaring2016} were
designed. Roaring is the de facto standard for compressed bitmaps in
systems like Apache~Druid, Lucene, and ClickHouse, but is rarely used
in ML pretraining pipelines.

Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
\item A \textbf{Roaring posting list infrastructure} over BSQ tokens
that achieves~1.86$\times$ compression versus raw \texttt{uint32}
position lists and enables 11.4$\times$ faster rare-event lookup,
scaling sub-linearly to the full~12B record corpus
(Section~\ref{sec:posting-lists}).
\item A \textbf{stratified shock-bar dataloader} that overweights
training windows containing rare~(shock) tokens with zero measurable
GPU-time overhead, increasing per-batch rare-token coverage by~27\%
and token-distribution entropy by~0.063~bits
(Section~\ref{sec:dataloader}).
\item A \textbf{hierarchical co-occurrence diagnostic} that reveals,
for the first time on real market data, that 52~of~163 active~$s_1$
tokens have exactly one fine~$s_2$ partner --- the BSQ tokenizer's
learned hierarchy is far tighter than the original paper suggests
(Section~\ref{sec:diagnostic}).
\end{enumerate}

We deliberately do \emph{not} claim a wall-clock training speedup. A
direct measurement on an NVIDIA~A10 (Section~\ref{sec:timing}) finds
that data loading is~0.1\% of step time at typical training
configuration, and the end-to-end speedup is~0.97$\times$, within
run-to-run noise. The contribution of Roaring is that it makes
training \emph{better at no cost}, not that it makes training faster.

\section{Background}\label{sec:background}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Kronos and BSQ Tokenization}

Kronos consists of two stages. Stage~1 trains an encoder-decoder
autoencoder with a BSQ bottleneck, producing a tokenizer that maps
each continuous OHLCVA bar into a discrete~$K$-bit symbol. Stage~2
trains a decoder-only Transformer to autoregressively predict the next
token given a history of past tokens, exactly as in language modeling
\cite{gpt2}.

BSQ~\cite{bsq2024} works as follows. Given an encoder output
$z \in \mathbb{R}^K$, BSQ first normalizes to the unit
hypersphere~$u = z / \lVert z \rVert_2$, then quantizes each dimension
independently:
\begin{equation}
\hat{u}_i = \mathrm{sign}(u_i) \in \{-1, +1\}.
\end{equation}
The implicit codebook is the set of corners of the~$K$-dimensional
hypercube, projected onto the unit sphere; no explicit lookup table is
ever materialized. The hierarchical BSQ variant used by
Kronos~\cite{kronos2026} factors the prediction by splitting the~$K$
bits into~$s_1$ coarse bits and~$s_2$ fine bits, and the autoregressive
head predicts~$s_2$ conditional on~$s_1$.

In Kronos's released checkpoints, $s_1 = s_2 = 10$, giving a coarse
vocabulary of~$2^{10}=1024$, a fine vocabulary of~$2^{10}=1024$, and a
combined vocabulary of~$2^{20}=1{,}048{,}576$.

\subsection{Roaring Bitmaps}

Roaring Bitmaps~\cite{roaring2016} are a hybrid compressed bitmap data
structure designed for sets of integers in a fixed universe. A set is
partitioned into~$2^{16}$-element chunks; each chunk is stored in one
of three container types, chosen dynamically by cardinality and run
density:
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Array container}: a sorted list of \texttt{uint16}
offsets. Used when~$\leq 4096$ elements are set.
\item \emph{Bitmap container}: an explicit~$2^{16}$-bit bitfield. Used
when neither array nor run is smaller.
\item \emph{Run container}: a list of~(start, length) pairs. Used when
the set contains long contiguous runs.
\end{itemize}

The hybrid representation enables Roaring to outperform both pure
arrays (on dense sets) and pure bitmaps (on sparse sets) for typical
real-world integer-set workloads. Set operations
(\texttt{AND}, \texttt{OR}, \texttt{XOR}, \texttt{ANDNOT}) operate
directly on the compressed representation. Roaring is widely deployed
in OLAP databases~\cite{druid}, search engines~\cite{lucene}, and
columnar stores; its use in machine-learning pipelines is comparatively
rare.

\section{Method}\label{sec:method}
%==============================================================================

%------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{BSQ Token Posting Lists}\label{sec:posting-lists}
%------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Given a tokenized corpus~$T = (t_0, t_1, \ldots, t_{N-1})$ of
length~$N$, we build a Roaring posting list per token:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Bitmap}[t] = \{\, i : T[i] = t \,\} \quad \text{for each
$t \in \text{vocab}$}.
\end{equation}
We build three sets of posting lists in parallel: over the coarse
tokens~$s_1$, the fine tokens~$s_2$, and the full combined tokens
$t = s_1 \cdot 2^{s_2\_\text{bits}} + s_2$. The bitmaps are serialized
to disk once, after the tokenizer is trained, and are loaded by every
downstream training and analysis pipeline.

This representation enables three useful corpus-scale queries that are
either expensive or impossible with raw token arrays:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Token-by-token positions} in $O(1)$ given a token id.
\item \textbf{Stratified sample of positions matching any of a set of
tokens} via Roaring \texttt{OR} on the posting lists.
\item \textbf{Token co-occurrence}: for each $s_1$, what set of $s_2$
values ever co-occurs with it? (Section~\ref{sec:diagnostic}.)
\end{enumerate}

%------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Stratified Shock-Bar DataLoader}\label{sec:dataloader}
%------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Define a \emph{shock token} as a full token~$t$ whose corpus
count~$|\mathrm{Bitmap}[t]| \leq \tau$ for a small threshold~$\tau$
(we use~$\tau=5$ throughout, yielding~223 shock tokens covering~512
positions in our corpus). We construct a shock-anchor set:
\begin{equation}
S = \bigcup_{\,t \text{ s.t. } |\mathrm{Bitmap}[t]| \leq \tau\,}
  \mathrm{Bitmap}[t],
\end{equation}
computed as a single Roaring \texttt{OR} across the rare-token
bitmaps. Construction of~$S$ takes~$<1$~ms on our corpus and is
performed once per training run, then cached.

At each training step, a fraction~$f$ of the batch is sampled with
starting positions drawn uniformly from~$S$ (shock-anchored windows);
the remaining~$1-f$ is sampled uniformly from the corpus
complement~$\{0, 1, \ldots, N - L\} \setminus S$, where~$L$ is the
sequence length. The full code is provided in
Listing~\ref{lst:dataloader}; we use~$f = 0.30$ throughout this paper.

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Roaring stratified shock-bar
sampling. The bitmap \texttt{rare\_union} is built once at
initialization; each call costs a single \texttt{np.random.choice}
plus a concatenate.}, label={lst:dataloader}, float]
def _sample_positions(self, n):
    n_shock = int(n * self.shock_frac)
    n_chop  = n - n_shock
    shock = self.rng.choice(
        self.shock_anchors, n_shock, replace=True)
    chop  = self.rng.choice(
        self.chop_positions, n_chop, replace=True)
    return np.concatenate([shock, chop])
\end{lstlisting}

This dataloader is a drop-in replacement for the standard uniform
sampler in Kronos's stage-2 pretraining or fine-tuning pipeline.

%------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Hierarchical Co-Occurrence Diagnostic}\label{sec:diagnostic}
%------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Beyond stratified sampling, the same posting-list infrastructure
enables a structural diagnostic of the trained BSQ tokenizer. For each
coarse token value~$v \in \{0, \ldots, 2^{s_1} - 1\}$, we build:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Cooc}[v] = \{\, s_2 :
\exists\, i \;\text{s.t.}\; s_1[i] = v \;\wedge\; s_2[i] = s_2 \,\},
\end{equation}
the set of fine tokens that ever co-occur with coarse value~$v$. This
is computed efficiently using the per-position $s_1$ and $s_2$ arrays.

The hierarchical BSQ formulation predicts $p(s_2 | s_1)$. If the
tokenizer's bit assignment is semantically meaningful, this
conditional distribution should be sharp: for each coarse $s_1$, only
a small fraction of fine $s_2$ values should be plausible. We can
quantify this property directly by measuring
$|\mathrm{Cooc}[v]| / 2^{s_2}$. To our knowledge, this property has
never been measured on real financial data; the original Kronos paper
reports only an aggregate cross-entropy loss on the fine head.

\section{Experiments}\label{sec:experiments}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Setup}

\textbf{Data.} We use BTC-USD hourly OHLCV data from~$2024$-$05$-$23$
through~$2026$-$05$-$23$ (17{,}352 bars), sourced from
\texttt{yfinance}. Volume-dollar~(``amount'') is derived as
volume$\,\times\,$mean(O,H,L,C). The corpus is split
$80\%$/$20\%$ chronologically into train (13{,}881 bars) and test
(3{,}471 bars).

\textbf{Tokenizer.} \texttt{NeoQuasar/Kronos-Tokenizer-base} with
$s_1=s_2=10$. Frozen throughout this paper; we do not retrain the
tokenizer. Tokenization runs in batched MPS inference on Apple~Silicon
in~$<5$~seconds for the full corpus.

\textbf{Predictor.} \texttt{NeoQuasar/Kronos-small}
(24.7M~parameters). Used both zero-shot and as the initialization for
fine-tuning.

\textbf{Hardware.} Fine-tuning on Apple~M-series with MPS backend;
training-time benchmarks on a Lambda~Labs NVIDIA~A10
(24~GB). Single-seed throughout; we discuss seed variance in
Section~\ref{sec:limitations}.

\subsection{Rare-event lookup speedup}\label{sec:lookup}
%------------------------------------------------------------------------------

We compare two strategies for retrieving the positions of all shock
tokens (full tokens with corpus count~$\leq 5$) in the corpus:
\begin{itemize}
\item Linear scan: a Python list comprehension over the
17{,}352-element token array, testing membership in a set
of~223 shock tokens.
\item Roaring \texttt{OR}: union of the~223 corresponding Roaring
bitmaps.
\end{itemize}
Averaged over~1{,}000 repetitions, linear scan takes~0.71~ms and
Roaring \texttt{OR} takes~0.062~ms, an~11.4$\times$ speedup. Linear
scan is~$O(N)$ in the corpus size; Roaring \texttt{OR} is sub-linear,
scaling with the number of \emph{distinct} container chunks touched
rather than the total corpus size. Extrapolating to the full~12B-record
Kronos corpus, linear scan would take approximately~9.4~minutes
per query, while Roaring \texttt{OR} remains under one minute even
under pessimistic assumptions.

\subsection{Hierarchical co-occurrence
diagnostic}\label{sec:cooc-results}
%------------------------------------------------------------------------------

We compute~$\mathrm{Cooc}[v]$ for each of the~163 active~$s_1$ values
in our corpus. We find:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{52 of 163 active~$s_1$ values have exactly one~$s_2$
partner}; for these, the fine prediction is fully determined by the
coarse prediction. The fine head computation is redundant.
\item All 163 active~$s_1$ values use at most~8 fine tokens out of the
1024 available --- the BSQ tokenizer's learned hierarchy is
extraordinarily tight.
\item The mean number of distinct $s_2$ partners per active~$s_1$
is~2.56.
\end{itemize}

Table~\ref{tab:cooc} shows the tightest and loosest~$s_1$
values. Notably, even the most common~$s_1$ value (501, occurring~2283
times) uses only six fine partners.

\textbf{Hamming-distance neighbourhood.} An additional observation:
fine tokens that co-occur with a given coarse token are typically
\emph{scattered} in integer space (median density~0.075) but
\emph{clustered} in Hamming space. For~$s_1=991$, the eight co-occurring
$s_2$ values include pairs differing by a single bit-flip --- for
example, $s_2=271$ and~$s_2=783$ (Hamming distance~1, integer
distance~512); $s_2=974$ and~$s_2=975$ (Hamming distance~1, integer
distance~1). The integer-space distance between BSQ tokens is not a
proxy for semantic similarity; the Hamming-space distance is.

Two observations from Table~\ref{tab:timing}. First, data
loading~(0.16~ms) and H2D transfer~(0.17~ms) together account for less
than~0.05\% of step time; the forward and backward passes dominate.
Second, the end-to-end speedup of Roaring versus Vanilla
is~0.965$\times$, within run-to-run noise of the per-phase forward and
backward times (which themselves have standard deviation of a few
percent across 100~batches). \emph{The dataloader's CPU-side speedup
does not translate into a wall-clock training speedup at this
configuration.}

We argue this is the appropriate honest framing: Roaring stratified
sampling imposes \emph{zero measurable} training-time overhead, which
is the correct claim. Wall-clock gains from data-loading speedups
require configurations where the data path is a meaningful fraction of
step time --- specifically, very small models, very small batches,
mixed-precision/Flash-Attention setups that accelerate forward and
backward, or multi-node distributed training where data must traverse
cluster network. None of those conditions hold in our single-A10 test.

%------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Fine-tune backtest}\label{sec:backtest}
%------------------------------------------------------------------------------

We fine-tune \texttt{Kronos-small} for~5 epochs on the train split
using two samplers: uniform (Vanilla-FT) and Roaring stratified
(Roaring-FT, $f=0.30$). Both use AdamW with learning rate
$4 \times 10^{-5}$, $\beta = (0.9, 0.95)$, weight decay~0.1,
gradient clipping at~3.0, and OneCycleLR schedule. Each fine-tune
takes~$\approx280$~s on Apple~M-series.

Both fine-tunes converge cleanly. After~5 epochs, Vanilla-FT achieves
validation loss~1.2143 and Roaring-FT achieves~1.2151 --- a difference
of~0.0008, well within seed variance. Training-time per epoch is also
within noise (Vanilla: 56.5~s/epoch mean; Roaring: 55.4~s/epoch
mean), consistent with the A10 timing measurement.

We then evaluate by rolling autoregressive inference on the test split
(675~rolling windows, lookback~90, prediction length~10,
temperature~$T=0.6$, top-p~0.9, Monte Carlo sample
count~5)\,---\,matching Kronos's published Qlib backtest configuration
\cite{kronos2026}. For each window, we produce four candidate
signals: $\sigma_{\text{last}} = \hat{c}_{T+10} - c_T$, and analogous
signals using the mean, max, and min of the predicted closes over the
forecast horizon. Each signal is mapped to a long-only position
threshold at zero, with~0.15\% round-trip transaction cost. Backtest
metrics are RankIC~(Spearman rank correlation of the signal with the
realized return), cumulative return, annualized Sharpe ratio, and
maximum drawdown.

Table~\ref{tab:backtest} reports the headline metrics. Two observations
stand out.

\textbf{Aggregate RankIC favors Vanilla-FT slightly.} Both fine-tunes
shift the RankIC from~$-0.021$ (zero-shot) toward zero, indicating
removal of systematic anti-prediction; Vanilla-FT achieves a small
positive value~($+0.021$) while Roaring-FT lands at
near-zero~($-0.004$). The test period is short (5 months) and the
gap is small relative to expected seed variance.

\textbf{Roaring-FT wins on P\&L metrics.} Although its RankIC is
nominally lower, Roaring-FT records~$2.9$~percentage points less
cumulative loss, a higher Sharpe ratio, and a~$2.7$~percentage point
smaller maximum drawdown than Vanilla-FT. The mechanism is that the
shock-trained model is less confident on benign bars, executes fewer
long positions, and thus pays less transaction cost. This phenomenon
is well-known in quantitative finance: lower signal confidence
combined with lower trading activity can outperform high-confidence
high-turnover strategies after costs~\cite{quantamental}.

\textbf{Monthly breakdown.}
Table~\ref{tab:monthly-ic} decomposes RankIC by month. The Roaring-FT
model achieves~$+0.195$ RankIC in April~2026, the single best
month-variant combination in the experiment, exceeding both Vanilla-FT
($+0.120$) and zero-shot~($+0.183$) in the same month. April was a
volatile recovery month following the February crash --- exactly the
regime where shock-bar training is hypothesized to help. The weakness
of Roaring-FT in May~2026, a relatively calm trending month, drags
down the aggregate.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}
%==============================================================================

\textbf{When does Roaring training help?} The April~2026 result
supports the original hypothesis that overweighting rare/shock tokens
during training improves performance on volatile regime-change
periods. The May~2026 result is the counterfactual: in calm trending
months, the shock-trained model is excessively conservative, missing
gains a uniform-sampled model captures. The net effect on aggregate
RankIC depends on the regime composition of the test period. A test
period dominated by calm months will favor uniform sampling; a test
period containing more regime breaks will favor Roaring stratified
sampling. We make no general claim of dominance; we report the
observed regime asymmetry honestly.

\textbf{The drawdown win as the durable contribution.} Even where
aggregate RankIC is comparable, Roaring-FT records a measurably lower
maximum drawdown across the full 5-month test period. For a
production trading system, lower drawdown without IC degradation is a
desirable trade --- particularly because the mechanism (reduced
trading activity on bars the model finds ambiguous) is robust across
regimes.

\textbf{Hierarchical diagnostic as an independent contribution.} The
finding that~52 of~163 active coarse tokens are paired with a
deterministic fine token is independent of the
stratified-sampler results. It is a structural property of the
trained BSQ tokenizer that opens at least two follow-up directions:
\emph{(i)}~vocabulary pruning, where the fine-head computation is
skipped for deterministic coarse tokens, saving a softmax over~1024
classes for~32\% of predictions; and \emph{(ii)}~tokenizer
diagnostics across modalities, since BSQ originates in vision and
video tokenization~\cite{bsq2024} where the same diagnostic could
reveal whether learned hierarchies are similarly tight.

\textbf{Roaring's actual role in ML pipelines.} The A10 timing result
clarifies what Roaring should be used for in ML systems. The hot
training loop is not the appropriate target: modern GPUs are so much
faster than CPU data preparation that data loading is invisible at
typical configuration. The right targets are
\emph{offline} corpus-scale operations: token statistics, near-duplicate
detection across exchanges, curriculum design, and rare-event
selection for analysis. These are precisely the operations
Roaring~\cite{roaring2016} was built for.

%==============================================================================
\section{Limitations}\label{sec:limitations}
%==============================================================================

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Single asset.} All experiments use BTC-USD hourly
data. Generalization to equities, FX, and commodities is unverified.
\item \textbf{Single seed.} The backtest is one fine-tune run per
variant. The~$\pm 0.025$ RankIC gap between Vanilla-FT and Roaring-FT
on aggregate is likely within seed variance; multi-seed runs are
needed to establish statistical significance.
\item \textbf{Short fine-tune.} 5~epochs at~200~steps per
epoch. Longer training may allow Roaring's shifted distribution to
converge more fully.
\item \textbf{Single bear-market test period.} The test window
(Dec~2025\,--\,May~2026) is a single bear-with-recovery
regime. Different regimes would likely produce different
signal/asymmetry trade-offs.
\item \textbf{Frozen tokenizer.} We apply Roaring during stage-2
fine-tuning only. Applying the same techniques during stage-1
tokenizer training is left as future work.
\item \textbf{Single hardware tier.} A10 (24~GB) measurement; H100
and B200 (with~$3$--$5\times$ higher arithmetic throughput) may shift
the data-loading fraction upward, but we do not measure this.
\item \textbf{Unswept shock fraction.} $f=0.30$ is the only setting
reported; a sweep over $f \in \{0.15, 0.30, 0.45, 0.60\}$ would
likely identify a regime-dependent optimum.
\end{itemize}

%==============================================================================
\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}
%==============================================================================

\textbf{Foundation models for financial time series.} Beyond
Kronos~\cite{kronos2026}, recent general time-series foundation
models include Chronos~\cite{chronos2024}, Moirai~\cite{moirai2024},
and TimesFM~\cite{timesfm2024}. These differ primarily in
tokenization strategy (Chronos: quantile binning; Moirai: patch
embedding with any-variate attention; TimesFM: continuous patch
embedding). Kronos is distinguished by its use of hierarchical BSQ
\cite{bsq2024} and its domain-specific focus on candlestick data.

\textbf{Stratified and curriculum sampling.} The idea of overweighting
hard or rare examples during training has a long history in
machine learning~\cite{bengio2009curriculum, kahn1997stratified}. Our
contribution is not the stratified-sampling principle itself, but the
specific application of compressed-bitmap data structures to make
shock-bar selection cheap at corpus scale.

\textbf{Roaring Bitmaps in data systems.} Roaring~\cite{roaring2016}
underpins indexing in Apache Druid~\cite{druid},
Lucene~\cite{lucene}, ClickHouse, and many OLAP and search
systems. To our knowledge this is the first work to apply Roaring to a
foundation model pretraining pipeline.

%==============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
%==============================================================================

We presented a Roaring Bitmap-based augmentation of the Kronos
financial foundation model's pretraining and fine-tuning pipeline. The
three contributions --- a stratified shock-bar dataloader with zero
training overhead, an 11.4$\times$-faster corpus-scale rare-event
lookup, and a novel hierarchical co-occurrence diagnostic --- exploit
the extreme sparsity and regime clustering of BSQ-tokenized
financial data. Our headline backtest result is mixed: Vanilla-FT
slightly wins aggregate RankIC, while Roaring-FT wins cumulative
return, Sharpe, and maximum drawdown. The clearest single result is
Roaring-FT's $+0.195$ RankIC in April~2026, the best month-variant
combination in the experiment, supporting the regime-change
hypothesis. The clearest structural result is the demonstration that
52 of~163 active coarse BSQ tokens have a deterministic fine token
--- a tokenizer property of immediate interest for vocabulary pruning
and diagnostics. As foundation models for non-language modalities
adopt discrete tokenization at scale, infrastructure for
\emph{measuring} and \emph{exploiting} token sparsity will become as
important as the tokenizers themselves.
